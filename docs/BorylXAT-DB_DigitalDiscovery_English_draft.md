# BorylXAT-DB: an IRC-validated transition-state database for machine-learning prediction of Lewis base–boryl radical C–Cl XAT barriers

## Abstract

Lewis base-coordinated boryl radicals (LBRs) provide a tunable main-group radical platform for halogen-atom transfer (XAT) activation of C–Cl bonds, but their reactivity is controlled by coupled variations in the boryl radical, Lewis base, and chlorinated substrate. Quantitative understanding of these effects has been limited by the absence of a large, reaction-specific, and systematically validated transition-state dataset. Here we report  **BorylXAT-DB** , an intrinsic-reaction-coordinate (IRC)-validated quantum-chemical database for LBR-mediated C–Cl XAT. The database is derived from an experimentally relevant reaction space comprising 55 boryl radicals, 386 Lewis bases, and 179 chlorinated substrates, corresponding to approximately 3.8 × 10⁶ possible component combinations. Through thermodynamic filtering, automated transition-state searches, frequency analysis, and bidirectional IRC validation, we obtained 9,237 validated transition states with complete reactant–transition-state–product mappings. Each entry contains optimized structures, thermodynamic and kinetic quantities, electronic descriptors, reaction mappings, and validation metadata, and the database is released in both ASE SQLite and Parquet formats to support reproducible atomistic inspection and scalable data-science workflows. Statistical analysis reveals that C–Cl activation is governed by a thermodynamic baseline defined by the balance between substrate C–Cl and product B–Cl bond dissociation free energies, giving rise to a clear Bell–Evans–Polanyi relationship between reaction and activation free energies. However, substantial dispersion around this relationship shows that transition-state geometry, steric accessibility, boron-centred charge and spin distribution, and orbital interactions provide additional kinetic control. A CatBoost model trained on mechanism-informed reactant- and product-derived descriptors achieves R² = 0.91 and a mean absolute error of 1.39 kcal mol⁻¹ in five-fold cross-validation, demonstrating quantitative barrier interpolation within the validated chemical space. BorylXAT-DB therefore provides both a benchmark resource for transition-state chemistry and a machine-learning-ready platform for mechanistic analysis, virtual screening, and data-driven design of main-group radical C–Cl activation.

## Introduction

Radical reactions provide mechanistic manifolds for bond activation and molecular reorganisation that are difficult to access through conventional polar pathways. Among them, halogen-atom transfer (XAT) offers a direct route from organohalides to carbon-centred radical intermediates that can participate in subsequent addition, reduction, coupling, or functionalisation reactions. C–Cl bonds are particularly challenging XAT substrates because they generally possess high bond dissociation free energies, are comparatively resistant to single-electron reduction, and often show limited reactivity under mild conditions. Nevertheless, chlorinated organic compounds are abundant in commodity feedstocks, pharmaceutical intermediates, and environmentally relevant molecules. Developing predictable and tunable strategies for C–Cl bond activation is therefore important for expanding the synthetic value of less-reactive chlorinated substrates.

Lewis base–coordinated boryl radicals, commonly referred to as Lewis base–boryl radicals (LBRs), have emerged as a distinctive main-group radical platform for organohalide activation. Coordination of an amine, phosphine, N-heterocyclic carbene, or related Lewis base stabilises the electron-deficient boryl radical while modulating the electronic structure, spin distribution, steric accessibility, and B–X bond-forming ability of the boron centre. Previous studies have shown that LBRs can mediate borylation reactions, hydrogen-atom transfer, and XAT processes, including the activation of alkyl iodides, alkyl bromides, and selected activated chlorides. In stepwise dechlorofunctionalisation of activated trichloromethyl compounds, appropriate Lewis base–borane precursors can even enable selective cleavage of a single C–Cl bond among several chemically similar C–Cl bonds.

Despite these advances, unactivated alkyl chlorides remain a central challenge in LBR chemistry. Recent studies have therefore explored reaction modes distinct from classical direct XAT, such as radical replacement, to overcome the intrinsic limitations of direct chlorine-atom abstraction. These observations suggest that LBR-mediated C–Cl XAT cannot be rationalised by substrate C–Cl bond strength alone. A key mechanistic question is therefore to what extent thermodynamic driving force determines the activation barrier, and how transition-state geometry, boron-centred electronic structure, Lewis-base donation, and steric accessibility modulate deviations from this thermodynamic baseline. Answering this question requires transition-state data that are broad enough to separate global thermodynamic trends from component-specific stereoelectronic effects.

Large-scale quantum-chemical datasets and open reaction databases are transforming the analysis of reaction mechanisms and reactivity trends. Structures and energies obtained at a consistent computational level provide a comparable basis for statistical mechanistic analysis, while machine-learning models can identify nonlinear structure–reactivity relationships and support barrier prediction, virtual screening, and reaction design. However, most existing open computational databases focus on ground-state molecular properties, generic organic reaction energetics, or broadly sampled transition-state collections. They do not provide a reaction-specific, component-resolved, and systematically validated transition-state dataset for a main-group radical XAT process in which the radical reagent, coordinating Lewis base, and chlorinated substrate are varied independently. To the best of our knowledge, no open database currently combines complete transition-state verification with machine-learning-ready descriptors for LBR-mediated C–Cl XAT. This data gap limits quantitative assessment of whether thermodynamic driving force alone is sufficient to predict barriers, and how structural, electronic, and steric factors contribute to deviations from a thermodynamics-only description.

Here we introduce BorylXAT-DB, a large-scale DFT transition-state database dedicated to LBR-mediated C–Cl XAT. The database is constructed from a chemically representative reaction space comprising 55 boryl radicals, 386 Lewis bases, and 179 chlorinated substrates, which together define approximately 3.8 × 10^6 possible component combinations. Through thermodynamic filtering, automated transition-state search, frequency analysis, and bidirectional intrinsic reaction coordinate (IRC) validation, we obtained 9,237 IRC-validated transition-state structures with associated reaction paths. Each entry connects the reactant complex, transition state, product complex, and corresponding carbon-centred radical with thermodynamic quantities, kinetic parameters, electronic and geometric descriptors, and validation metadata. The database is released in both ASE SQLite and Parquet formats to support atomistic inspection, reproducible statistical analysis, and direct integration with machine-learning workflows.

Using BorylXAT-DB, we examine how thermodynamic driving force, transition-state structure, and component-level stereoelectronic effects jointly govern LBR-mediated C–Cl XAT barriers. The resulting analysis establishes a pronounced Bell–Evans–Polanyi relationship between reaction free energy and activation free energy, while also identifying systematic deviations associated with boron-centred electronic structure, steric environment, and transition-state geometry. A CatBoost model trained on mechanism-informed descriptors provides an interpretable baseline for activation-barrier prediction and shows that multidimensional molecular information substantially improves upon a thermodynamics-only description. More broadly, BorylXAT-DB provides a reaction-specific benchmark for transition-state chemistry, a reusable data model for main-group radical reactions, and a machine-learning-ready platform for barrier prediction, mechanistic benchmarking, and data-driven exploration of C–Cl activation strategies.

## Dataset Generation and Database Construction

### Reaction-space design

This work focuses on the elementary chlorine-atom transfer step underlying C–Cl bond activation by Lewis base-coordinated boryl radicals (LBRs). In this XAT step, an LB–boryl radical species abstracts a chlorine atom from an organochloride substrate, affording the corresponding Lewis base-coordinated chloroborane and a dechlorinated carbon-centred radical:

LB–B• + R–Cl → LB–B–Cl + R•

Because the activation free energy and thermodynamic driving force of this step depend jointly on the boryl radical, Lewis base, and chlorinated substrate, the initial reaction space was constructed along these three component axes. Rather than exhaustively enumerating all possible LBR-mediated C–Cl activation reactions, we aimed to define an experimentally motivated and chemically diverse reaction space for mechanistic analysis and data-driven model development. The molecular components were selected from commercially available, synthetically accessible, or experimentally plausible candidates; complete molecular lists and classification rules are provided in the Supporting Information.

The resulting component library comprises 55 boryl radicals, 386 Lewis bases, and 179 chlorinated substrates. The boryl-radical set includes parent, monosubstituted, and disubstituted boryl-radical motifs. The Lewis-base set covers N-based donors, phosphines, and N-heterocyclic carbenes, representing major donor families in Lewis base–borane and boryl-radical chemistry. The chlorinated-substrate set includes carbon tetrachloride, trichloromethyl substrates, dichloromethyl substrates, and monochlorinated compounds with chlorine bound to either sp³- or sp²-hybridised carbon centres.

Across these component classes, substituents and molecular scaffolds were chosen to span the electronic and steric variations most relevant to C–Cl XAT. These include alkyl, alkenyl, aryl, halogen, amino, alkoxy, cyano, carbonyl-containing substituents, and heterocyclic groups. This design enables systematic assessment of how substituent and scaffold effects modulate boron-centred radical character, Lewis-base donor strength, B–Cl bond formation, substrate C–Cl bond strength, and steric accessibility at the reaction centre. The chlorinated substrates span a wide range of Gibbs-free-energy-based C–Cl bond dissociation free energies (BDFEs), approximately 40–110 kcal mol⁻¹, covering thermodynamically labile polychlorinated substrates as well as comparatively inert monochlorinated substrates.

Combining these 55 boryl radicals, 386 Lewis bases, and 179 chlorinated substrates defines an initial combinatorial reaction space of approximately 3.8 × 10⁶ possible LBR-mediated C–Cl XAT reactions. This reaction space served as the starting point for thermodynamic filtering, transition-state searches, IRC validation, and database construction.

### Automated DFT workflow and transition-state validation

BorylXAT-DB was generated using a hierarchical automated quantum-chemistry workflow combining molecular mechanics, semiempirical conformational sampling, DFT geometry optimisation, transition-state search, IRC validation, and single-point energy refinement. Full computational settings, convergence thresholds, and validation criteria are provided in the Supporting Information.

Initial three-dimensional structures were generated from SMILES strings or structural templates using RDKit and pre-optimised with the UFF force field. For conformationally flexible species, CREST/GFN2-xTB conformational sampling was performed, and low-energy, geometrically diverse conformer candidates were retained through RMSD-based filtering before DFT optimisation.

All ground-state species, including isolated components, LBR reactant complexes, Lewis base-coordinated chloroborane products, and dechlorinated carbon-centred radical products, were optimised at the B3LYP-D3(BJ)/6-31G(d)/SMD(toluene) level and verified as minima by vibrational frequency analysis. Thermal corrections from these frequency calculations were used to obtain Gibbs free energies at 298.15 K and 1 atm. For open-shell species, retrospective analysis of the computed ⟨S²⟩ values gave a range of 0.751–0.832, suggesting that severe spin contamination was not observed for the doublet radical species considered in this dataset.

Transition-state initial guesses were automatically constructed from predefined XAT geometries by placing the transferring chlorine atom between the boron and carbon centres. These guesses were refined through conformational sampling, constrained optimisation, and Berny saddle-point searches. Candidate transition states were required to exhibit exactly one imaginary vibrational frequency, with a normal mode corresponding to coupled C–Cl bond cleavage and B–Cl bond formation.

All candidate transition states were further subjected to bidirectional intrinsic reaction coordinate (IRC) calculations. The IRC endpoints were evaluated using automated bond-length and connectivity criteria to confirm that each transition state connected the expected reactant-like and product-like endpoints of the XAT pathway. Structures that failed the single-imaginary-frequency criterion, showed an inconsistent imaginary mode, converged to reactant- or product-like structures during saddle-point optimisation, or failed IRC endpoint validation were excluded from the final database. Thus, the released transition-state subset contains only successfully optimised, frequency-verified, and IRC-validated entries.

To obtain a higher-level energetic description, single-point electronic energies were computed for all optimised structures at the ωB97X-D/6-311+G(d,p)/SMD(toluene) level. Final Gibbs free energies were obtained by combining these single-point electronic energies with thermal corrections from the B3LYP-D3(BJ) frequency calculations. This composite protocol was selected based on benchmarking against DLPNO-CCSD(T)-based reference barriers with continuum-solvation corrections, as detailed in the Supporting Information. The benchmark subset was curated to cover the major boryl-radical, Lewis-base, and chlorinated-substrate classes represented in the database.

For each validated XAT pathway, activation and reaction free energies were defined using the Gibbs free energies of the mapped reactant and product species:

ΔG‡ = G_TS − G_LB–B• − G_R–Cl

ΔG_rxn = G_LB–B–Cl + G_R• − G_LB–B• − G_R–Cl

Unless otherwise noted, all Gibbs free energies were obtained using the same composite energy protocol. To interpret the thermodynamic driving force for chlorine-atom transfer, Gibbs-free-energy-based bond dissociation free energies (BDFEs) were calculated as:

BDFE_C–Cl = G_R• + G_Cl• − G_R–Cl

BDFE_B–Cl = G_LB–B• + G_Cl• − G_LB–B–Cl

Thus, the overall XAT reaction free energy can be expressed as the difference between the C–Cl and B–Cl BDFEs:

ΔG_rxn = BDFE_C–Cl − BDFE_B–Cl

This formulation relates the XAT reaction free energy directly to the balance between the free-energy cost of C–Cl bond cleavage and the free-energy gain from B–Cl bond formation.

### Thermodynamic filtering and transition-state data selection

Because transition-state optimisation across the full combinatorial reaction space is computationally prohibitive, the initial space was reduced by thermodynamic filtering before transition-state calculations were performed. The filtering procedure focused the high-throughput workflow on candidates for which both LBR complex formation and the XAT step are thermodynamically favourable.

First, all 21,230 possible boryl radical–Lewis base combinations were evaluated for complex formation:

LB + B• → LB–B•

Only complexes with a complexation free energy ΔG_complex < −4.1 kcal mol⁻¹ were retained. At 298.15 K, this threshold corresponds approximately to an equilibrium constant greater than 10³ for the corresponding association process, providing a practical criterion for selecting LBR complexes that are expected to be significantly populated under the modelled conditions. This step retained 20,010 LBR complexes and discarded 1,220 combinations.

Second, the 20,010 retained LBR complexes were combined with the 179 chlorinated substrates to generate a candidate reaction pool. The reaction free energy was then evaluated for each candidate XAT reaction:

LB–B• + R–Cl → LB–B–Cl + R•

Only reactions satisfying ΔG_rxn < −4.1 kcal mol⁻¹ were retained. This second filter applies the same free-energy threshold to the XAT reaction, focusing subsequent transition-state calculations on candidates with a favourable thermodynamic driving force. After this filtering step, 3,040,617 candidate reactions were retained.

From this thermodynamically favourable reaction pool, a subset of approximately 15,000 reactions was selected for automated transition-state calculations to keep the computational scale tractable. After transition-state optimisation, frequency analysis, and bidirectional IRC validation, 9,237 transition states were accepted into BorylXAT-DB. Failed or unvalidated calculations were excluded from the released dataset. The final transition-state subset therefore constitutes a curated collection of IRC-validated C–Cl XAT reaction paths within the thermodynamically favourable region of the designed LBR reaction space.

### Database schema and access

BorylXAT-DB was designed as a reaction-path database rather than a collection of isolated transition-state geometries. Each validated reaction entry stores explicit mappings among the LBR reactant complex, chlorinated substrate, transition state, Lewis base-coordinated chloroborane product, and dechlorinated carbon-centred radical product. This reaction-centred data model allows the full reaction free-energy profile to be reconstructed from the database records.

The database contains the component and endpoint structures required to reconstruct the reaction network: 55 boryl radicals, 386 Lewis bases, 179 chlorinated substrates, and the corresponding 179 dechlorinated carbon-centred radical products. It also contains 20,010 LBR reactant complexes, 20,010 Lewis base-coordinated chloroborane product complexes, and 9,237 IRC-validated transition states. Each structure is assigned a unique identifier, and each transition-state entry stores component identifiers, reactant and product structure keys, activation and reaction free energies, the imaginary vibrational frequency, IRC endpoint information, and reaction-mapping metadata.

The dataset is released in two complementary formats. The first is an Atomic Simulation Environment (ASE) SQLite database, in which molecular structures are stored as ASE Atoms objects together with searchable metadata and associated data fields. This format is suitable for atomistic inspection, reaction-path reconstruction, structural visualisation, and follow-up quantum-chemical calculations. The second is a flattened Parquet dataset designed for large-scale statistical analysis and machine-learning workflows. This machine-readable tabular representation contains structure identifiers, component labels, molecular information, atomic coordinates, thermodynamic quantities, geometric parameters, and electronic descriptors, and can be read directly by common Python data-analysis libraries.

The ASE and Parquet formats are linked through shared structure and reaction identifiers, enabling users to move programmatically between tabular analysis and atomistic structure inspection. The accompanying code repository provides example scripts for loading the database, reconstructing reaction pathways, extracting descriptors, reproducing the main statistical analyses, and training the baseline machine-learning models. Complete field definitions, naming conventions, validation metadata, and example queries are provided in the Supporting Information.

## Results and Discussion

We next characterize the validated transition-state subset of BorylXAT-DB and illustrate its use for statistical analysis and machine-learning-based barrier prediction. Unless otherwise stated, all analyses in this section are based on the 9,237 transition-state entries that passed frequency analysis and bidirectional intrinsic reaction coordinate (IRC) validation. The discussion is intended to define the chemical and energetic scope of the released resource, identify statistically supported structure–energy relationships, and provide a baseline demonstration of how the database can be used in data-driven modelling workflows.

### Chemical coverage and thermodynamic landscape of BorylXAT-DB

We first analysed the component-level coverage of the final IRC-validated transition-state set. Because this subset was obtained after thermodynamic filtering, random selection for transition-state calculations, automated saddle-point searches, and IRC validation, it should not be viewed as an unbiased sample of the full combinatorial reaction space. Instead, its composition defines the validated region of BorylXAT-DB for which complete reaction-path information is available. Assessing this composition is therefore necessary for interpreting the scope and limitations of the subsequent statistical analyses.

The validated set covers all three component axes considered in the database design: boryl radicals, Lewis bases, and chlorinated substrates. For the boryl-radical component, BH₂•-derived systems account for 8.7% of the entries, while monosubstituted and disubstituted boryl radicals account for 64.2% and 27.1%, respectively. Thus, the final dataset is not dominated by the parent boryl radical, but contains substantial variation in the substitution environment around the boron centre.

The chlorinated-substrate distribution also spans multiple C–Cl bonding environments. Reactions involving carbon tetrachloride account for 2.3% of the validated entries, trichloromethyl substrates for 19.4%, dichloromethyl substrates for 22.8%, and monochlorinated substrates for 55.5%. The dataset therefore includes both highly activated polychlorinated substrates and less activated monochlorinated compounds. This coverage is important because the intrinsic difficulty of C–Cl activation depends strongly on chlorine substitution pattern, carbon hybridization, and substituent effects.

The Lewis-base component includes the major donor families represented in the initial library. N-based Lewis bases account for 63.8% of the validated reactions, phosphines for 19.8%, and N-heterocyclic carbenes (NHCs) for 16.3%. These families provide different donor strengths and steric environments around boron, allowing the database to sample how Lewis-base coordination modulates boron-centred radical character, chlorine affinity, and reaction-site accessibility. Taken together, these distributions show that the validated subset retains chemically useful coverage across the principal component classes, while also reflecting the filtering, sampling, and validation criteria used to construct the database.

The thermodynamic data further indicate that BorylXAT-DB covers a broad range of C–Cl and B–Cl bond strengths. The substrate C–Cl bond dissociation free energies (BDFEs) span approximately 40–110 kcal mol⁻¹, ranging from labile polychlorinated substrates to high-BDFE aryl and vinyl chlorides. The distribution is strongly differentiated by carbon hybridization: sp³ C–Cl bonds generally populate lower-BDFE regions, whereas sp² C–Cl bonds are shifted toward higher BDFEs. This trend is consistent with the combined effects of hybridization, substituent electronics, and radical stabilization on the free-energy cost of C–Cl bond cleavage.

The B–Cl BDFEs of the Lewis base-coordinated chloroborane products span approximately 60–120 kcal mol⁻¹ and are stratified by Lewis-base family. This distribution indicates that the Lewis base affects not only the stability of the boryl-radical precursor, but also the thermodynamic gain associated with B–Cl bond formation. Consequently, the reaction free energy for XAT can be interpreted as the balance between the cost of C–Cl bond cleavage and the gain from B–Cl bond formation. The comparison of substrate C–Cl BDFEs and product B–Cl BDFEs therefore provides a useful thermodynamic descriptor for assessing the feasibility of LBR-mediated C–Cl activation.

### Thermodynamics, transition-state geometry, and XAT reactivity

The relationship between reaction free energy, ΔG_rxn, and activation free energy, ΔG‡, provides a global measure of how thermodynamic driving force is reflected in kinetic accessibility. Across the 9,237 IRC-validated transition states, ΔG_rxn and ΔG‡ show a pronounced Bell–Evans–Polanyi (BEP) relationship, with a Pearson correlation coefficient of 0.73. More exergonic reactions generally have lower computed activation barriers, consistent with a chlorine-transfer coordinate in which C–Cl cleavage and B–Cl formation are coupled.

At the same time, the BEP relationship does not provide a complete description of barrier variation. A linear model using only ΔG_rxn gives R² ≈ 0.53, indicating that reaction thermodynamics alone account for only about half of the variance in ΔG‡. The residuals from this fit have a standard deviation of 4.37 kcal mol⁻¹, which is sufficiently large to motivate analysis beyond a one-parameter thermodynamic model. These deviations suggest that component-specific structural, electronic, and steric effects contribute to the activation barrier in addition to the overall C–Cl/B–Cl BDFE balance.

Transition-state geometry provides an independent way to evaluate the character of the validated XAT pathways. Most transition states exhibit a near-linear B–Cl–C arrangement, with B–Cl–C angles concentrated in the 160–180° range. This geometry is consistent with the expected halogen-atom-transfer motif, in which the transferring chlorine atom lies between the carbon and boron centres. The concentration of IRC-validated structures in this angular range also supports the chemical consistency of the automated transition-state search and validation workflow.

The B–Cl and C–Cl distances at the transition state quantify the progression of bond formation and bond cleavage along the chlorine-transfer coordinate. The B–Cl distance is moderately anticorrelated with ΔG‡, with Pearson r = −0.49, indicating that shorter B–Cl distances at the transition state are associated with lower computed barriers. In contrast, the C–Cl distance is moderately correlated with ΔG‡, with Pearson r = 0.44, suggesting that more extensive C–Cl elongation is associated with higher barriers in a subset of later or more structurally reorganized transition states. The B–Cl–C angle shows a weaker anticorrelation with ΔG‡, with Pearson r = −0.18. Thus, near-linear alignment appears to be a common geometric feature of validated XAT transition states, whereas barrier variation is more closely associated with the relative extent of B–Cl formation and C–Cl elongation.

Together, the thermodynamic and geometric analyses indicate that LBR-mediated C–Cl XAT barriers follow a dominant thermodynamic trend but are not determined by reaction free energy alone. The BEP relationship defines a useful baseline associated with C–Cl/B–Cl BDFE matching, while the remaining dispersion reflects additional sensitivity to local transition-state structure and component-specific electronic and steric environments. This interpretation motivates the use of multidimensional descriptors to encode reactivity information beyond ΔG_rxn.

### Mechanism-informed descriptors and machine-learning barrier prediction

To demonstrate the machine-learning utility of BorylXAT-DB, we constructed a mechanism-informed descriptor set and trained a CatBoost gradient-boosting regression model to predict DFT-derived activation free energies. The purpose of this model is not to introduce a new algorithmic architecture, but to provide a transparent baseline for evaluating how well chemically interpretable descriptors reproduce the computed reaction manifold.

The descriptor set was organized around four chemically relevant species in the XAT step: the Lewis base-coordinated boryl radical reactant complex (LB–B•), the chlorinated substrate (R–Cl), the Lewis base-coordinated chloroborane product complex (LB–B–Cl), and the carbon-centred radical product (R•). These species encode boron-centred radical character, substrate C–Cl bond activatability, B–Cl bond-forming thermodynamics, and carbon-radical stabilization, respectively. The 18 descriptors include reaction free energy, selected reactant and product bond lengths, Hirshfeld charges, spin densities, frontier orbital energies, dipole moments, and buried volumes. Importantly, no transition-state-derived descriptor was used as a model input; all features can be assembled from reactant and product species and their associated thermodynamic quantities stored in the database.

Descriptor correlation analysis shows that ΔG_rxn is the single descriptor most strongly associated with ΔG‡, consistent with the BEP relationship described above. However, the remaining descriptors encode electronic and steric information that is not fully captured by reaction free energy. The largest pairwise Pearson correlation among descriptors is approximately 0.895, indicating moderate internal correlation but no severe multicollinearity for the purpose of baseline regression modelling.

Using the 18 descriptors, the CatBoost model achieves R² = 0.91 and a mean absolute error of 1.39 kcal mol⁻¹ in random five-fold cross-validation. Repeating the five-fold procedure with different random seeds gives highly consistent performance, showing that the reported accuracy is not specific to a single random partition. Relative to the ΔG_rxn-only linear model, the improved performance indicates that reactant and product electronic structure, bond metrics, and steric descriptors provide complementary information for predicting computed barriers within the validated database.

Feature-importance analysis provides a qualitative indication of the variables used most strongly by the model. ΔG_rxn is the most influential descriptor, in agreement with its dominant role in the BEP analysis. Other important descriptors are associated with substrate C–Cl bonding, boron-centred charge and spin distribution, frontier orbital energies, and buried volume. These features are chemically consistent with the geometric analysis and with the presence of BEP residuals: the activation barriers are largely organized by thermodynamics, but individual reactions deviate from this baseline according to local electronic and steric factors.

The random cross-validation results should be interpreted as an interpolation benchmark within the BorylXAT-DB chemical space. This setting is relevant for database use cases in which users query reactions drawn from the same validated component manifold. However, because related boryl radicals, Lewis bases, and chlorinated substrates may appear across different random folds, random cross-validation does not by itself quantify extrapolation to entirely unseen reaction components or component classes. More stringent component-disjoint and class-based validation protocols are therefore provided in the Supporting Information to assess model transferability beyond the random-split setting.

Overall, these analyses show that BorylXAT-DB functions not only as a collection of IRC-validated transition-state structures, but also as a machine-learning-ready reaction resource. Its consistent quantum-chemical energetics, explicit reactant–transition-state–product mappings, physically interpretable descriptors, and baseline predictive model make it suitable for statistical mechanistic analysis, barrier interpolation, virtual screening within the sampled chemical space, and future benchmarking of more transferable models for main-group radical reactivity.

## **Conclusions**

BorylXAT-DB provides a large-scale, reaction-specific quantum-chemical transition-state resource for C–Cl halogen-atom transfer mediated by Lewis base-coordinated boryl radicals. Starting from an experimentally relevant and chemically representative reaction space comprising 55 boryl radicals, 386 Lewis bases, and 179 chlorinated substrates, we used a hierarchical DFT workflow that integrates thermodynamic screening, random sampling, automated transition-state searches, frequency analysis, and bidirectional IRC validation to obtain 9,237 validated transition-state structures with complete reaction-path information. The database is released in both ASE SQLite and Parquet formats and contains atomic structures, thermodynamic and kinetic quantities, electronic descriptors, reaction mappings, validation metadata, and example analysis code. This dual-format design enables both atomistic inspection and scalable data-science workflows, thereby supporting reproducible reaction-path reconstruction, statistical analysis, and machine-learning model development.

Statistical analysis of BorylXAT-DB reveals that LBR-mediated C–Cl activation is governed by a thermodynamic baseline defined by the balance between substrate C–Cl BDFE and product B–Cl BDFE. This balance gives rise to a clear Bell–Evans–Polanyi relationship between ΔG_rxn and ΔG‡, establishing reaction free energy as the dominant first-order descriptor of the activation barrier. However, the substantial dispersion around this relationship demonstrates that thermodynamics alone is insufficient to describe the full barrier landscape. Transition-state geometry, steric accessibility, boron-centred charge and spin distribution, and orbital interactions provide additional kinetic control and rationalise deviations from the BEP trend. Consistent with this mechanistic picture, a CatBoost model trained on mechanism-informed reactant- and product-derived descriptors achieves R² = 0.91 and a mean absolute error of 1.39 kcal mol⁻¹ in five-fold cross-validation. These results show that BorylXAT-DB can support quantitative barrier interpolation and virtual screening within the validated chemical space, while also providing a chemically interpretable basis for analysing structure–reactivity relationships in main-group radical XAT chemistry.

Beyond this specific reaction class, BorylXAT-DB establishes a reusable data model for main-group radical reaction chemistry. By combining IRC-validated transition states, complete reactant–transition-state–product mappings, a consistent computational protocol, and machine-learning-ready descriptors, the database provides a benchmark platform for transition-state prediction, reaction-path search, transferable barrier modelling, and assessment of lower-cost quantum-chemical methods. Future extensions to C–Br, C–I, and other main-group radical XAT processes, together with explicit-solvent modelling and integration with automated experimental screening, should further broaden the mechanistic and predictive utility of this framework. In this way, BorylXAT-DB contributes not only a curated dataset for a challenging C–Cl activation problem, but also a general strategy for transforming main-group radical reactivity from empirical exploration into data-driven rational design.
